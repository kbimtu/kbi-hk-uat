About KBI Hong Kong

Our Impact

Numbers tell part of the story. The rest is in where people went, what they built, and what they said about the experience.

KBI Hong Kong has been running emerging-technology education in Hong Kong since 2016. This page gathers the most honest evidence we have of what that work has produced — not as a credential reel, but as a record of real people, real programmes, and real outcomes.

Our Measure of Impact

We Don’t Count Eyeballs.
We Track What Changes.

KBI Hong Kong is not a content platform. We do not measure our impact through follower counts, event attendance headcounts, or promotional impressions. Our programmes are structured, demanding, and slow — by design.

When we talk about impact, we mean something specific: whether a student learned to think more carefully about a complex technology. Whether they left a competition better at defending a design than when they walked in. Whether what they built here became the seed of something they carried into a degree, a lab, a company, or a career.

That kind of impact is harder to measure — and harder to fake.

Programme Impact

Three Tracks. One Common Standard.

Across blockchain, data science, and quantum computing, KBI Hong Kong runs structured competitions and training programmes that ask students to do something difficult in front of people who know the subject. Not difficult in a purely academic sense — difficult in the way that real work is difficult: incomplete information, time pressure, judges who will push back, and a team to hold together.

What students take out of our programmes is not just knowledge of these technologies. It is a practised ability to reason clearly about systems, communicate honestly about uncertainty, and defend positions under scrutiny. Those habits transfer.

Blockchain · IBCOL HK

Thinking About Blockchain Without the Hype

IBCOL HK challenges teams to design blockchain-based systems that could actually work — not just sound interesting. Judges are practitioners and researchers who care about architecture, feasibility, and the ethics of design choices.

Alumni consistently describe the distinction between what a technology is portrayed as and what it actually is as one of the most durable lessons they carried out.

Data Science · IDSOL HK

Learning to Work With Data That Doesn’t Behave

IDSOL HK gives teams real, noisy datasets and asks them to find something honest and useful within them. Students experience the gap between what they expect data to show and what it actually shows — under time pressure, with rigour and intellectual honesty.

Alumni from IDSOL often say the competition was the first time they genuinely grappled with messy data in a setting where the analysis actually had to be right, not just completed.

IDSOL HK details →
Quantum Computing · IQCOL HK

Early Exposure to Frontier Science

IQCOL HK invites students into one of the most technically demanding subjects in contemporary science before most universities offer a proper course in it — then explain their work to judges who will not pretend to understand something they don’t.

For many students, IQCOL is proof of concept: evidence that they can sit inside a genuinely hard topic, ask good questions, and not give up when the ideas resist easy summary.

Alumni Stories

Where Our People Went
and What They Built

KBI Hong Kong cannot claim responsibility for anyone’s degree, career, or company. What we can say, honestly, is that a consistent pattern shows up in the trajectories of people who engaged seriously with our programmes: they tend to move with more direction, more confidence in defending technical ideas, and a clearer sense of what they want to build.

The stories below are from Hong Kong students and alumni who passed through our programmes. We present them not as marketing, but as evidence — each one an individual trajectory where KBI HK appears as one influence among many.

Alumni Profile

Dominic Tam

Programmes: IBCOL 2022 · IBCOL 2023 · IDSOL 2024 · KBI Internship 2024
Current path: Candidate for Bachelor of Medicine, The University of Hong Kong · HKAGE · Pui Kiu College

Dominic entered KBI’s competition ecosystem as a secondary student and became one of the most decorated alumni across KBI-affiliated programmes. His blockchain teams earned a Gold Medal and Award of Distinction at IBCOL 2022, as well as a Top GovTech Award and Top Secondary Track Award at the Greater Bay Area Blockchain Olympiad. In 2024, his IDSOL team designed HealthNutz — a health-tech proposal combining AI computer vision with Traditional Chinese Medicine — earning a Silver Medal at the international stage.

After competing, Dominic joined KBI as a Blockchain Technology and Policy Researcher intern. His first project examined blockchain-based intellectual property protection for a global semiconductor company operating within the regulatory environment of the PRC. He produced actionable analysis of State-approved platforms and helped inform the client’s adoption strategy — applied policy research, not classroom exercises.

Now studying medicine at HKU, Dominic represents one of the clearest illustrations of what KBI’s programmes can do when a student is willing to engage fully: the skills built in competition rooms and internship settings transfer across disciplines, and the habit of defending ideas under expert scrutiny does not disappear at the end of an olympiad.

“The competitions helped me develop strong system design thinking skills, enabling me to approach complex problems with innovative solutions. I also improved my pitching and communication abilities, learning how to present ideas clearly and confidently.”

Nikkia Lo

IBCOL 2021 (2LUX) · IBCOL 2022 (IMProof)

BSc Marketing Analytics and Technology candidate, The University of Hong Kong · Diocesan Girls’ School

Nikkia came into KBI’s programmes without any particular intention of working in technology. She left with team leadership experience, system architecture skills, and a track record in project management — including co-organising DGS Demo Day 2022 — that she describes as foundational to her academic direction. Her participation in IBCOL directly changed where she applied to university.

“Initially, I did not consider a career in innovation and technology. IBCOL convinced me to apply to tech programmes at Waterloo, HKU, and HKUST.”

Daniyar Kuzekov

LETTI 2024 · IBCOL 2024 (EduChain · Amsterdam Finals)

BSc Data Science, Lingnan University · Nazarbayev Intellectual School

Daniyar joined LETTI 2024 and progressed to represent Hong Kong at IBCOL 2024 in Amsterdam as a finalist on Team EduChain. His technical development fed directly into industry work — including a Data Engineering Apprenticeship at Thinkpanda and Web3 development for Atomic Knowledge Proof, building front-end systems for IBCOL with measurable impact on participation.

“I learned a lot about blockchain technology, attended multiple fruitful networking events, broadened my horizons — and travelled to Amsterdam.”

Nigel Gurung

TETTI 2025 · ETO 2024 (Conviction) · KBI × THEi Internship 2025

BEng Civil Engineering (Honours) candidate, THEi

A civil engineering student, Nigel entered through TETTI and ETO 2024, joining the Hong Kong delegation to Amsterdam with Conviction — a decentralised subscription network using blockchain for digital identity verification. His Research Track internship applied systems thinking to a strategic marketing audit targeting Gen Z and Gen Alpha audiences.

Chillie Wong

TETTI 2025 · ETO 2024 · KBI × THEi Internship 2025 (Event Production)

BSc Surveying Technology candidate, THEi · CIC BIM Competition 2023 Champion

Chillie joined the Hong Kong delegation to Amsterdam through IBCOL 2024 and TETTI, then served as an Event Production intern — designing materials, coordinating logistics, and analysing participant feedback. Her path shows that students from built-environment disciplines bring distinct strengths to emerging-technology education.

Sakib Rabbani

IBCOL 2021 (UniMatrix)

Software Engineer, JP Morgan HK · Founder, Talentier (Silicon Valley)

Sakib credits the KBI and IBCOL network with connecting him to industry professionals who led to his first engineering role at JP Morgan HK. He later founded Talentier, a startup he has since brought to Silicon Valley.

“The IBCOL, IDSOL, and KBI network connected me with industry professionals, which helped me get a job in Software Engineering at JP Morgan HK.”

Sophia Liu

IBCOL 2021 (HelpProof) · IBCOL 2024 (ClubProof)

Diocesan Girls’ School · Lawrenceville School

Sophia joined IBCOL as a secondary student and stayed in the ecosystem for years, returning to compete in 2024. Her most durable takeaway: blockchain is not primarily a financial instrument, but a mechanism for establishing trust between parties who cannot otherwise verify each other.

“Never just look at what a technology is portrayed as; look at what a technology actually is and what it can do beyond what others think it can do.”

Aniruddha Ganguly

IBCOL 2020 (Health Ledger) · IBCOL 2021 (NetCredit)

Bangladesh University of Engineering and Technology · Stony Brook University

Aniruddha competed at IBCOL representing Bangladesh across two editions. Afterwards, he was selected for the Advanced Blockchain Development Internship — supervised by KBI subject matter experts and organised with Bangladesh’s Ministry of ICT through the Bangladesh Computer Council. His trajectory — from student competitor to government-partnered internship to graduate study at Stony Brook — shows how KBI’s network extends beyond the competition floor.
